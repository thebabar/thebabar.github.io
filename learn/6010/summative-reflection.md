# Summative Reflection: 

Coming from an engineering and data background, I found this course to be different from what I was used to. At first, it was hard for me to read the textbook. The theories seemed too abstract and theoretical. Slowly, that changed. As I re-read the book, watched the videos, and participated in discussions, I realized that these big questions do have a place. The Dimensions of Influence model was especially helpful because it gave me a way to ground my view of learning technology. I came to understand why philosophy sits at the top of the model. It shapes the assumptions underneath everything else.

Across the argument papers and reflections, I have come to see instructional technology as a field that cannot be separated from theory, philosophy, ethics, cognition, and design. At the beginning, it is easy to think of learning technologies mainly as tools: platforms, AI systems, modules, simulations, assessments, or content delivery systems. But the deeper lesson from this course is that every educational technology carries assumptions. It carries assumptions about what knowledge is, how people learn, what counts as evidence, what values matter, and whose needs are prioritized. Because of that, instructional technology is never just technical. It is theoretical, philosophical, and ethical.

One major theme for me is the recognition of importance of theory, even with its limitations. Theory does not give perfect answers, it becomes outdated, and it cannot fully capture the complexity of human learning. Learners are different. Contexts are different. Motivation, identity, prior knowledge, culture, and social conditions all shape how people learn. Still, theory matters because it gives us a way to see what we might otherwise miss. Without theory, instructional design can easily become a set of surface-level practices: make a course, add a quiz, track completion, and call it learning. Theory helps us ask better questions. What kind of learning are we supporting? What assumptions are built into the technology? What evidence would actually show understanding? What are the limits of our design?

Cognitive Load Theory has been especially useful for me because it connects theory directly to design decisions. CLT reminds us that learners have limited working memory, and that instructional design can either support or overload cognition. This matters for microlearning, AI-supported learning, and professional education. A poorly designed course can increase extraneous load through cluttered slides, vague explanations, too much content, or irrelevant tasks. A better design manages complexity, sequences information carefully, uses examples, and gives learners practice at the right level. For me, CLT supports the idea that learning for busy professionals should be focused, relevant, and designed around what learners can realistically process and apply. 

At the same time, CLT alone is not enough. Learning is not only cognitive processing. It is also contextual, social, ethical, and practical. Constructivist theory and the social construction of knowledge helped me recognize that learners do not simply receive knowledge. They interpret it through experience, prior assumptions, identity, and dialogue with others. This is especially true with generative AI. People can learn about AI from readings or lectures, but they cannot develop real AI fluency through explanation alone. They have to use AI, test prompts, see mistakes, revise outputs, compare responses, and develop judgment through experience. However, this does not mean learners should be left alone without guidance. My view is that learners need both structure and experience. They need credible knowledge, but they also need authentic practice and feedback. It took a while to figure all this out.

This connects directly to my philosophical bias. My philosophical bias in instructional technology is pragmatic, cognitively informed, and critically aware. I believe learning requires credible knowledge and structured support, but meaningful learning does not occur through static content exposure alone. Learning becomes valuable when learners can apply ideas in realistic contexts, receive timely feedback, and make better decisions in new situations. This bias comes from my experience with corporate training and professional learning. I have seen many training programs that are technically complete but educationally weak. Slide-based modules, compliance courses, and multiple-choice checks may document participation, but they often fail to improve understanding, judgment, or transfer.

Because of this, I am skeptical of instructional technologies that prioritize volumes of content, just completion, tracking, or superficial engagement over meaningful application. I am more interested in designs that help learners think, practice, receive feedback, and transfer knowledge to real situations. This is why microlearning interests me, but only when it is designed well. Microlearning should not mean shallow learning or fragmented content. Its value is that it can reduce unnecessary cognitive burden, fit into the real constraints of adult learners, and support ongoing reinforcement. When microlearning is connected to a coherent learning path, realistic scenarios, feedback, and spaced practice, it can support deeper learning over time.

AI creates both opportunity and risk in this context. Used appropriately, AI can support personalized explanations, scenario generation, feedback, practice, and reflection. It can help learners move from passive content consumption to active engagement. For example, an AI system can help a learner practice a workplace conversation, evaluate an AI-generated output for errors, or apply a concept to a realistic business problem. But AI can also weaken learning if it simply gives answers, encourages passive copying, or hides mistakes behind fluent language. The appropriate use of AI in instructional technology should develop learner judgment, not replace it. AI should help learners ask better questions, test ideas, identify limitations, and make better decisions.

Ethics is central to this. Throughout the course, I have become more aware that educational technology is not neutral. Technologies can support access, personalization, and feedback, but they can also reinforce bias, surveillance, inequality, and shallow definitions of learning. My strongest ethical priorities are equity and trust. Learning technologies should work for diverse learners, not just those who already know how to succeed in formal systems. They should be transparent, accurate, safe, and accountable. This is especially important with AI because learners may trust systems that sound confident even when they are wrong. Ethical instructional design requires human oversight, transparency about limitations, protection of learner data, and attention to who benefits or is harmed by a system.

Simulations have also become an important part of my thinking. Because we cannot directly observe what is happening inside another person’s mind, simulations can help us observe richer evidence of thinking-in-action. Instead of only asking learners whether they understand a concept, we can place them in realistic situations and see how they apply knowledge, make decisions, respond to feedback, and adapt. Simulations are especially useful for professional learning because they can approximate real-world complexity without the same risks or costs. For example, a learner studying AI ethics can evaluate a flawed vendor proposal, respond to a hallucinated AI output, or make a decision about whether to deploy an AI tool in a sensitive context.

However, simulations also have limits. A simulation is still a designed environment, not reality itself. It reflects the assumptions of its designers. AI-based simulations can generate useful practice and possible scenarios, but synthetic data from AI agents should not be treated as direct evidence of human behavior. Human-in-the-loop simulations are stronger because they involve real learners making decisions in structured situations. Even then, we are still interpreting evidence. We do not have direct access to learning itself. We have traces of learning: explanations, choices, artifacts, performance, revisions, and reflections. I intend to spend more time on designing better simulations.

This connects to the broader limits of social science. Social science can function as a science, but not as a science of perfect certainty. In education, we cannot directly access another person’s thoughts, beliefs, motives, or understanding. We infer learning from evidence. Assessment is not a direct window into the mind, but it is often the best practical tool we have, short of observing a person across many real-world situations over many years. The challenge is not to reject assessment, but to design better assessments. Stronger assessments use authentic tasks, multiple forms of evidence, learner explanations, scenarios, feedback loops, and opportunities for transfer. They do not eliminate uncertainty, but they support more responsible inferences.

For me, this means instructional design must be both ambitious and humble. It should be ambitious because good design can improve learning, support transfer, reduce cognitive load, create meaningful practice, and expand access. But it should also be humble because human learning is complex. No single theory, platform, assessment, or AI system can fully capture what learners know or who they are becoming. Good instructional design requires theory, but it also requires judgment. It requires us to ask what the theory helps us see and what it may cause us to overlook.

Overall, this course has helped me connect my practical concerns about corporate training, AI learning, and microlearning to deeper theoretical and philosophical questions. I still believe learning should help people act more effectively in real situations. But I now see more clearly that designing for application requires more than practical instincts. It requires theories of cognition, knowledge, identity, ethics, and evidence. Instructional technology should not simply deliver content faster or measure activity more efficiently. It should help learners build understanding, practice judgment, receive feedback, and apply knowledge responsibly. Theory matters because it gives us a disciplined way to design for that goal, even while reminding us that all educational claims must be made with care, context, and humility.
