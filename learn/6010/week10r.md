# Week 10 Reflection: 

Rosenberg's book gave me an interesting perspective on the complexitites of social sciencees, the challenge of definiing it as a science and how philosophy is relevant and unavoidable in the debate. It is clear that social sicence is in its own categroy, different from hard sciences. In educational technology, we cannot directly access what is in learners’ minds, so we must infer learning from behavior, language, performance, reflection, and context. This makes research more difficult. Most of the qualitative research papers I read have also convinced me of this. It means our designs, assessments, and claims must be careful, limited, and open to revision. I also believe that careful use of AI will help with getting over some limitations of the traditional research methods in social science and learning technologies. 


In education, we never have direct access to another person’s mind. We can observe behavior, read written responses, listen to explanations, examine performance (digitally or otherwise), and ask learners to reflect, but all of these are indirect indicators. This creates a major challenge for instructional design, assessment, and research.

The problem of other minds is especially important because learning is not only external behavior. A learner may answer correctly without deep understanding. Another learner may misunderstand a concept but still perform well on a narrow task. A third learner may understand something deeply but struggle to express it in the format required by an assessment. This means that educational researchers and instructional designers have to be careful about claiming that a tool “caused learning” or that an assessment fully measures understanding. What is the best measure for long-term performance and can it come from assessments? This is an open question.

The goal of science is to develop disciplined and methodical ways of asking questions, gathering evidence, testing explanations, and revising claims. In social science, this means using systematic methods while recognizing that human behavior is shaped by cognition, context, culture, motivation, identity, and social conditions. Social science can produce useful knowledge, but that knowledge is often probabilistic, contextual and subjective.

This has direct implications for educational technology. If we cannot fully know what is happening in learners’ minds, then learning technologies should not rely too heavily on a single form of evidence. For example, an LMS may show that a learner completed a module, but completion does not prove understanding. A quiz score may show recall, but it may not show transfer. Time-on-task may show engagement, but it can also show confusion, distraction, or inefficiency. Learning analytics come with their own limitations and don't tell the full story. 

Instructional design is a new field that I need to learn more. Learners differ in prior knowledge, language, working memory, motivation, cultural background, confidence, and goals. A design that works well for one learner may overwhelm another. For this reason, effective educational technologies should include multiple forms of support: clear structure, examples, practice, feedback, reflection, and opportunities for learners to explain their thinking. They should also allow some flexibility, because not all learners process information in the same way. However personalization has remained elsuive despite attempts of many and this also needs further research. I am reminded of the novely effect that was discussed many times in the class.

Assessment systems should also be designed carefully. If the goal is to measure whether learners can apply knowledge in real situations, then multiple-choice questions may not be enough. Scenario-based tasks, simulations, written explanations, think-aloud activities, portfolios, and feedback-rich practice can provide richer evidence. Even then, assessment remains an inference. We are interpreting signs of learning, not directly seeing learning itself. This means good assessment should combine multiple indicators and avoid overclaiming.

Different philosophies affect how researchers handle this uncertainty. A positivist approach may emphasize observable behavior, measurement, and statistical patterns. This can be useful when studying large groups or comparing interventions, but it may miss learners’ internal reasoning and lived experience. An interpretivist approach may focus more on meaning, context, and learner perspective. This can reveal how learners understand their own experiences, but it may be harder to generalize. A critical perspective asks how power, culture, bias, and institutions shape what counts as knowledge and whose learning is valued. I think educational technology research benefits from using more than one lens, because each philosophy notices something different.

I am especially interested in whether learners can make better decisions in new situations. That kind of learning cannot be fully captured by clicks or completion data. It requires authentic tasks, explanation, practice, feedback, and reflection. Simulations can improve social science research by making it possible to study behavior in controlled, repeatable, and ethically safer environments. In educational technology, simulations are especially valuable because they can reveal how learners make decisions, apply knowledge, respond to feedback, and adapt in realistic situations. However, AI-generated simulations should not be treated as direct substitutes for human participants. They are best used for hypothesis generation, scenario testing, instrument design, and exploratory modeling. Claims about human learning still require validation with real learners. The role of simulations and their design has to be studied further. What assumptions were made, what was measured and whether the simulated sitatuion represented real human learning.
