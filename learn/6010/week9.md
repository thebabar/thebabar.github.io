# Week 9 Reflection: 

Let me start by sharing a reflection on Constructivism and Generative AI. As I reviewed Duffy and Cunningham’s constructivist view, I realized that their work is especially relevant to modern generative AI because AI fluency cannot be developed through passive explanation alone. Learners can be introduced to concepts such as prompting, hallucination, bias, and model limitations through readings or lectures, but they only begin to understand these ideas deeply when they interact with AI systems directly. By experimenting with prompts, evaluating outputs, noticing errors, revising instructions, and comparing results across contexts, learners construct practical understanding of how generative AI behaves. This supports the constructivist claim that knowledge is actively built through experience, interpretation, and meaningful activity rather than simply transmitted from expert to learner.


At the same time, this does not mean learners should learn AI completely alone. Social discussion matters because learners benefit from comparing experiences, sharing prompting strategies, questioning assumptions, and discussing ethical concerns. I have seen this at many in-person workshops at Dallas AI (and in online forums to some extent s well). In this sense, AI learning is both individual and social: learners construct understanding through direct interaction with AI, but they refine that understanding through dialogue with peers, instructors, and communities of practice.


On Identity-Sharing Exercises:
The identity-sharing process helped me recognize that my approach to learning, teaching, and research is shaped by more than academic theories. It is also shaped by my lived experiences as a learner, professional, educator, and builder of learning technologies. I learned that I tend to view education through a practical and critical lens. I care about whether learning actually helps people understand, apply, and make better decisions, especially in professional contexts. This comes from seeing many examples of training that looked complete on paper but did not meaningfully change understanding or performance.

One thing I learned about myself is that I value relevance strongly. I do not see learning as simply receiving information or completing assigned activities. I believe learning becomes meaningful when people can connect ideas to their own context, test those ideas through practice, and receive feedback that helps them improve. This connects with constructivist theory because learners are not passive recipients of knowledge. They bring their prior experiences, assumptions, culture, and goals into the learning process. At the same time, I do not believe learners should be left completely on their own to construct meaning. My view is that learners need credible knowledge, structure, and guidance, but they also need opportunities to make sense of that knowledge through authentic activity and dialogue.

The identity-sharing process also helped me see how my professional background influences my future growth. As nuch of my experience comes from corporate training, AI education, and professional learning, I naturally focus on transfer, application, and decision-making. I tend to ask: How will this work in a real situation? Can they make a better judgment because of what they learned? Can they recognize mistakes or limitations? This gives me a useful lens as an educator and researcher, but it also creates a limitation. I may sometimes overvalue immediate practical application and undervalue theoretical exploration for its own sake. Recognizing this helps me grow because it reminds me that theory is not separate from practice. Theory can help explain why certain practices work, when they work, and where they may fail. Without theory, it is hard to present evidence and have a foundation for arguments.

My identity also influences how I think about instructional technology. I am skeptical of technologies that prioritize completion, tracking, or superficial engagement. For example, a slide-based compliance course with a short multiple-choice quiz (the corporate standard) may document that someone participated, but it may not show whether they can apply the concept responsibly in a real situation. This is especially important with AI learning. If an AI tool simply gives learners answers, it may create passive use rather than deeper understanding. However, if AI is used to create scenarios, provide feedback, support reflection, and help learners practice better judgment, then it can support meaningful learning. This reflects my belief that technology is not neutral. It carries assumptions about what knowledge is, what counts as learning, and what kind of learner behavior matters.

Values and beliefs intersect with theory and research because researchers do not approach education from nowhere. Our questions, methods, interpretations, and conclusions are shaped by what we believe matters. If I believe learning is mainly about recall, I will design different assessments than if I believe learning is about transfer and judgment. If I believe technology is neutral, I may ignore the hidden assumptions built into platforms. If I believe technology reflects power, access, and design choices, I will ask different research questions about equity, trust, and learner agency. This identity-sharing process made that more visible to me.

I also believe identity comes from multiple sources. It comes from family, culture, education, work, faith, community, and repeated experiences over time. Identity is not only personal. It is also socially shaped. The way people see themselves as learners depends partly on how others respond to them, what opportunities they receive, what institutions value, and what kinds of knowledge are treated as legitimate. This connects directly to the social construction of knowledge. What people know and how they learn are shaped through interaction, dialogue, feedback, and participation in communities. This makes things complicated for sure but this is the reality.

As an educator, this means I should not treat learners as blank slates or identical users of a system. They come with histories, strengths, constraints, motivations, and identities that shape how they interpret instruction. As a researcher, it means I need to be aware of my own assumptions and how they influence the questions I ask. As someone interested in instructional technology, it means I should design and evaluate learning systems not only for efficiency, but also for meaning, trust, agency, and transfer.

Overall, the identity-sharing process helped me see that my philosophy of learning is both personal and theoretical. I believe learning should be structured, credible, active, contextual, valuable and reflective. My experiences have made me critical of shallow training and optimistic about learning designs that combine high-quality knowledge with authentic practice and feedback. This reflection also reminded me that my own identity is still developing. As I continue growing as an educator and researcher, I want to stay open to perspectives that challenge my assumptions while continuing to focus on learning that helps people think, act, and make better decisions in real situations.
